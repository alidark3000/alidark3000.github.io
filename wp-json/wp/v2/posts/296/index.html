Hi Homies. In this case study you’ll see a brand strategy take shape and flourish as messaging, style guidelines, and more specific tactics for content and web. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

When I first got into brand strategy, it blew my mind, but the process I learned was a little rigid. The longer I do it, the more I find my own way. My process is very front-loaded my comparison, with much more emphasis on motivations, origins and stories. Changing up the process means re-testing, while looking for a project to get stuck into I remembered that game I spent a few years making – Glitch Arena. It’s a perfect candidate on so many levels…<\/p>\n\n\n\n

Glitch is loved by the few who play it, but never got the design treatment it deserved, let alone the marketing. The company we created to launch it, Shock & Rockets, is little more than a shell – completely ready to be recreated in whatever image I see fit. Glitch is more or less a hobby project, so the stakes are low, but there is still potential to see how a brand affects engagement. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

Starting with WHY<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

This is almost a meme at this point, but a it’s a universal concept in design. I’ve outlined a WHY above, but a few others became clear. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

    \n
  • Capitalise on, and highlight, work already done.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
  • Attract studio interest for possible partnerships.<\/li>\n\n\n\n
  • Introduce the game to people who might love it, or even benefit from it.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

    These reasons set intentions, create context, and also become reference points to help develop a brand.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

    Futures<\/h2>\n\n\n\n

    This section is adapted from Futures Studies, and its purpose is to identify a preferred future, backcast a path to the present and work out what changes need to be made to reach this future. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

    The preferred future of Shock & Rockets is provide for a small, likely dispersed, team. It would achieve this by having one initial commercial success, then reinvesting into future indie projects. It would allow the founders (me and Joel) to focus on game design and testing and not be involved in technical or production. There are no specific changes required to reach this future. <\/p>\n\n\n\n

    Futures Studies also identifies trends that might help or hinder that path. The main trends affecting Glitch are<\/p>\n\n\n\n

      \n
    • Bifurcation of games: \n
        \n
      • large, conglomerate games like Fortnite, which contain many mini-games with great accessibility, with experiences that can be easily shared, and monetisation over time<\/li>\n\n\n\n
      • small, indie games with shorter, more personal experiences, genre-entires and entry fees. <\/li>\n<\/ul>\n<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

        This trend reveals a need to drill down on what is good about indie games and avoid trying to replicate experiences from larger games:<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          \n
        • a more personal experience. <\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • genre advancing entries that entice an existing market<\/li>\n\n\n\n
        • quality of experience over length and scale<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n\n\n\n

          On “Personal”<\/strong> experiences in games<\/h4>\n\n\n\n

          A personal experience means one that can be fully enjoyed without others. The most acclaimed single player games take players on emotional journeys, like Celeste. Others are centred on likeable characters and compelling stories. In our case, personal is more likely to relate to the gameplay. In the later brand attributes section you’ll see how we define ourselves as being down-to-earth, creating cerebral games that require method and planning (another way of saying “hard” in non-mechanical ways). In this way a player is engaging with the level and obstructions. But still, some work to work on player goals, other than getting through the level, will be needed because this doesn’t come second nature to us<\/strong>. This is likely to be something whimsical and fun, rather than emotional, which could work well with the genre. Successful modern indie single player FPS games include Dusk, where ether player is discovering and escaping a cult, and Ion Fury.<\/p>\n\n\n\n

          On genre entries<\/h4>\n\n\n\n

          Another core insight within those is the one relating to genre. We are firmly rooted in 90s first-person-shooter genre. We’ve eliminated multiplayer for future projects because it requires a large audience and the quality of the experience largely depends on the quality of the audience. That leaves single player FPS games. To create a genre advancing (same but better in a key way) game that people want to play might require being more formulaic in the areas that are not <\/em>different, in order to have the genre appeal.
